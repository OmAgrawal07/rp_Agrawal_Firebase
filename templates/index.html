Effect of Cereal Plant Staws' Breakdown on Anabaena inaequalis

O. Agrawal, Dr. D. Ellsworth

Background

A process known as eutrophication has been slowly developing over the years, impacting our environment. What is eutrophication? It is the process by which algae builds up in bodies of water due to excess nutrients such as nitrogen and phosphorus. These sudden algae blooms in water can have detrimental effects, including greenhouse gas emissions. Due to a build-up of algae, rather than releasing oxygen, these cyanobacteria deprive the environment of oxygen and release carbon dioxide. This causes the suffocation of aquatic organisms and a destabilizing effect on the ecosystem. The main natural methods that have been studied to slow down the growth of algae was the use of cereal plant straws, or more specifically, barley straws. Barley straw works to slow down algae growth through a series of decomposition, resulting in hydrogen peroxide which in turn, stops continual algal growth.

Rationale

Previous research has mainly focused on testing with barley straw, as we know. My research aimed to expand our knowledge of other cereal plant straws, including rye straw, wheat straw, and orchard grass hay. The study tested for oxygen levels, carbon dioxide levels, and absorption of light. Through these tests, the study will determine which cereal straw may have a greater impact on algae growth, positively or negatively. Using the data from the study, we can determine which cereal straws may come in more use to add to greater bodies of water in order to halt the process of eutrophication and stablize aquatic environments for animal and plant life alike.

Hypothesis & Prediction

My hypothesis was that changing cereal straw types may affect the algae population growth rate as well as oxygen and carbon dioxide levels in closed environments. I predicted that the algae population growth rate and carbon dioxide levels would significantly decrease. In contrast, oxygen levels would significantly increase.
